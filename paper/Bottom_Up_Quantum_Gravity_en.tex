\begin{abstract}

We propose a minimal framework in which space, time, and gravity emerge
from the entanglement structure of a finite global quantum state,
without any pre-existing spacetime background.

Geometry is reconstructed from mutual information between subsystems
and projected via multidimensional scaling. The effective spatial
dimension depends on the entanglement structure and transitions between
different regimes according to the locality of correlations.

Several operational signatures are demonstrated numerically on systems
of $N=9$ and $N=16$ qubits, and further extended through a first
phenomenological exploration on galaxies from the SPARC sample:

\begin{itemize}
\item relational time via modular flow,
\item emergent geometry via informational distance reconstruction,
\item thermodynamic gravity via a Jacobson-type entropy-energy relation,
\item intrinsic geometry via spectral dimension,
\item wormhole-like ER=EPR signatures,
\item chaotic modular dynamics characterized by random matrix theory,
\item horizon-like informational bottlenecks in entanglement graphs,
\item local response of discrete Ollivier--Ricci curvature to an
      informational perturbation,
\item robust selection of an effective U(1)-like symmetry in the
      effective vacuum,
\item quasi-local modular Hamiltonians as state-dependent geometric probes,
\item exploratory phenomenological extension to galactic rotation curves
      from the SPARC sample via an emergent effective dimension.
\end{itemize}

We also identify a topology-dependent modular constant
\[
C = d_A\, g_2^{\mathrm{plateau}}
\]
revealing that the modular Hamiltonian spectrum retains geometric
information about the underlying entanglement structure.

The Ollivier--Ricci curvature analysis provides the clearest numerical
signal obtained so far for a coupling of the form
\[
\text{local informational defect}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\text{emergent geometric curvature},
\]
with a response that grows with perturbation intensity and is more
localized for compact defects.

A first phenomenological extension to the SPARC galactic sample
suggests that a single-phase description is not the most natural:
current fits point toward distinct effective regimes, with optimal
dimensions
\[
d_{\min}^{\mathrm{massive}} = 2.4871 \pm 0.0294,
\qquad
d_{\min}^{\mathrm{dwarf}} = 2.2744 \pm 0.0341.
\]

We further show that the effective transition scale $r_{\mathrm{trans}}$
is not an arbitrary parameter but an observable correlated with the
baryonic and geometric structure of galaxies. A global multivariate
scaling law, followed by regime separation, leads to a falsifiable
prediction independent of rotation curves: the radial structure of
the lensing excess differs between galactic regimes.

This part should be interpreted as a phenomenological proof of concept
linking bottom-up emergent geometry to real astrophysical data.

\end{abstract}

\section{Central Idea}

\begin{quote}
Entanglement is a geometric structure.

What we call space is the collective pattern of these correlations.

What we call gravity is the thermodynamics of their reorganization.
\end{quote}

The Bottom-Up approach postulates that spacetime is not fundamental
but emerges from the internal correlation structure of quantum states.

\section{Minimal Postulate}

We assume the existence of a global pure quantum state

\[
|\Psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H} = \bigotimes_{i=1}^{N} \mathcal{H}_i
\]

defined over $N$ elementary degrees of freedom.

No spacetime structure is assumed. There is:

\begin{itemize}
\item no metric,
\item no spatial manifold,
\item no external time,
\item no classical gravitational field.
\end{itemize}

All geometric and dynamical notions must emerge from the correlations
within $|\Psi\rangle$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{BuP positioning.}
Within the Bottom-Up (BuP) framework developed in this work,
no background geometry, no effective Hamiltonian, and no gauge
structure is postulated. Instead, geometry, dynamics, and symmetry
are all derived directly from the entanglement structure of a finite
quantum state $|\Psi\rangle$. This distinguishes BuP from approaches
such as AdS/CFT or tensor networks, where an ambient geometry or
a bulk Hamiltonian is assumed at the outset.

\[

\[
O(\tau)=e^{iK_A\tau}\,O\,e^{-iK_A\tau}.

w_{ij}=I(i:j).

\[
L=D-W

\[
Z(\tau)=\mathrm{Tr}(e^{-\tau L}).

The spectral dimension

\[
d_s(\tau)=-2\frac{d\log Z}{d\log\tau}

gives

\[
d_{IR}\approx2.5\text{--}2.7.

\delta S\approx \beta_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\delta E.

\section{Discrete Curvature: Testing Strategy}

We seek to determine whether a local informational perturbation can
act as a source of emergent curvature.

A first family of tests based on discrete triangular holonomies and
local loop-type observables did not yield a robust curvature signal.
This approach remains conceptually useful for probing the local graph
structure, but failed to establish a reproducible matter--curvature
coupling.

We therefore introduced a more stable discrete curvature measure:
the Ollivier--Ricci curvature computed on the entanglement
graph \cite{ollivier}.

\section{Discrete Ollivier--Ricci Curvature}

To test more directly the hypothesis
``informational matter $\rightarrow$ curvature'',
we replaced the triangular curvature diagnostic by a transport
measure on the entanglement graph: the Ollivier--Ricci curvature.

\subsection{Test principle}

From the mutual information matrix, we reconstruct a fixed-density
entanglement graph. A local informational perturbation is then
introduced in a source region $[5,6]$, with controlled radius $r$
and intensity $s$.

We define the mean curvature variation

\[
\Delta \kappa = \kappa_{\mathrm{pert}}-\kappa_{\mathrm{base}},
\]

and the locality observable

\[
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{near-far}}
=
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{near}}
-
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{far}}.
\]

A positive value of $\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{near-far}}$ means that
the geometric response is stronger near the perturbed region than
at distance.

\subsection{Connectivity control}

The local perturbation may modify not only the curvature but also
the graph topology itself. To separate these two effects, several
comparison modes were tested. The most robust is the
\texttt{frozen\_baseline} mode, in which the reference graph
connectivity is held fixed while curvature is re-evaluated after
perturbation. This allows the observed signal to be interpreted as
an intrinsic curvature response rather than a mere topological
rearrangement effect.

\subsection{Multi-seed result at $r=1$, $s=0.6$}

Over a batch of 20 seeds for $N=16$, the \texttt{frozen\_baseline}
mode gives

\[
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{edge}} = 0.0758 \pm 0.0296,
\]

with positive fraction $=1.00$, and

\[
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{near-far}} = 0.0927 \pm 0.0799,
\]

positive in $95\%$ of seeds.

A local informational perturbation thus increases the mean graph
curvature, and this increase is on average stronger near the source
than at distance.

\subsection{Intensity response law}

We performed an intensity scan
\[

\subsection{Radius effect}

A joint scan over intensity and radius shows that:

A compact source thus curves more locally, while an extended source

Fits on the intensity scans show that the best effective model is

\[
\Delta \kappa(s)\approx as^2+bs+c,

The response is therefore not strictly linear: it first grows with
perturbation intensity, then tends toward saturation.

\subsection{Interpretation}

This result constitutes the clearest signal obtained so far in the

\[
\text{local informational defect}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\text{emergent geometric curvature}.
\]

This is not yet a discrete Einstein equation derived from first
principles, but a clear numerical proof of concept showing that
informationally-defined ``matter'' can act as a source of curvature
on the entanglement graph.

\section{Effective Vacuum Symmetry in BuP}

The Bottom-Up program has so far established consistent signatures
of emergent geometry, modular dynamics, and matter--curvature coupling
within entanglement graphs. We investigate here a complementary
question: does the effective vacuum of the model select a residual
symmetry structure?

\subsection{Effective model}

We consider an XXZ-type Hamiltonian with weak local random fields:

\[
H_{\mathrm{BuP}} =
- J_{zz}\sum_i Z_i Z_{i+1}
- J_{xy}\sum_i (X_iX_{i+1}+Y_iY_{i+1})
+ \sum_i (h_i^x X_i + h_i^y Y_i + h_i^z Z_i).
\]

This model postulates no gauge symmetry a priori, but allows us to
test whether a collective structure emerges in the vacuum.
We note that the anisotropy $J_{zz} \neq J_{xy}$ already breaks $SO(3)$
explicitly at the Hamiltonian level; the objective is therefore not
to demonstrate spontaneous symmetry breaking in the field-theoretic
sense, but to test whether a robust effective residual symmetry
is selected in the ground state.

\subsection{Discrete gauge connection}

From the ground state, we extract the Pauli correlation tensor
between qubit pairs:

\[
C_{ij}^{ab} = \langle \sigma_a^i \otimes \sigma_b^j \rangle, \quad a,b \in \{x,y,z\}.
\]

The polar SVD decomposition of $C_{ij}$ (a $3\times3$ matrix) gives:

\[
C_{ij} = S_{ij} \cdot R_{ij}, \quad R_{ij} \in SO(3).
\]

The rotational part $R_{ij}$ defines a discrete gauge connection
on the qubit graph. The Lie algebra generated by $\{\log R_{ij}\}$
is analyzed via SVD to classify the effective symmetry.

\subsection{Observables}

We measure:

\begin{itemize}
\item the angular order parameter $OP = \langle |\cos\theta_{ij}| \rangle$,
      where $\theta_{ij}$ is the angle between each Lie vector and
      the dominant axis $\hat{n}$,
\item the singular ratio $\mathrm{ratio}_s = s_1 / s_2$
      (singular values of the Lie vector matrix),
\item the angular dispersion $\sigma_\theta$.
\end{itemize}

Classification criterion:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{U(1)-like} regime: $\mathrm{ratio}_s > 2.5$ and
      $\sigma_\theta < 0.4$ rad,
\item \textbf{SO(3)-like} regime: otherwise.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Main result}

For parameters $N=8$, $J_{zz}=2.0$, $J_{xy}=0.5$, $h_{\mathrm{scale}}=0.1$:

\begin{itemize}
\item $95\%$ of disorder realizations classified as \textbf{U(1)-like},
\item mean order parameter $OP \approx 0.969$ versus
      $OP_{\mathrm{random}} \approx 0.619$ for control random states.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stability with respect to the kNN graph}

A scan over the kNN parameter $k \in \{1,2,\ldots,7\}$
confirms the robustness of the signal:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}

\midrule

Random control states yield $0\%$ U(1)-like classification for all $k$.
This establishes that the effective symmetry is an intrinsic property
of the ground state $|\psi_0\rangle$, not an artifact of graph

\subsection{Vacuum uniqueness}

The dominant axis $\hat{n}$ is extracted for each disorder realization.
The angular dispersion between vacua is measured by:

\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{vac}} = \langle \mathrm{angle}(\hat{n}_i, \hat{n}_j) \rangle_{i \neq j}.
\]

Results ($N=8$, $h=0.1$, 30 seeds):

\begin{itemize}
\item $\Delta_{\mathrm{vac}} = 0.056$ rad (normalized: $0.072$, where
      $1$ corresponds to isotropic $S^2$),
\item alignment $\langle |\cos z| \rangle = 0.999$: the axis $\hat{n}$
      points quasi-perfectly in the $J_{zz}$ anisotropy direction,
\item sharp transition observed at $J_{zz}/J_{xy} \approx 1$:
      below, the vacuum is degenerate ($\Delta_{\mathrm{norm}} > 1$);
      above, it is unique.
\end{itemize}

The effective vacuum is therefore unique and oriented: it selects
the direction of microscopic anisotropy.

\subsection{Spontaneous vs explicit symmetry breaking test}

To distinguish axis selection due to explicit Hamiltonian anisotropy
from a potential spontaneous breaking, we performed the following test:

\[
H(\varepsilon) = H_{\mathrm{iso}} + \varepsilon \cdot H_{\mathrm{break}}(\hat{n}_{\mathrm{break}} \text{ random}),
\]

where $H_{\mathrm{iso}}$ is the isotropic Heisenberg Hamiltonian
(exact $SO(3)$) and $\hat{n}_{\mathrm{break}}$ is a random breaking
direction, different for each realization.

Result: $\mathrm{angle}(\hat{n}, \hat{n}_{\mathrm{break}}) = 0$ for
all $\varepsilon \in [0.001, 1.0]$, confirming that the axis selection
is of \textbf{explicit} rather than spontaneous nature. This result is
honest and expected: the anisotropy $J_{zz} > J_{xy}$ already breaks
$SO(3)$ at the Hamiltonian level.

The correct claim is therefore: \textit{the BuP gauge connection
constitutes a geometric observable sensitive to vacuum symmetry,
regardless of whether that symmetry is explicit or spontaneous.}

\subsection{Dependence on disorder and anisotropy}

Scan over $h_{\mathrm{scale}}$:

\begin{itemize}
\item U(1)-like regime stable for $h \lesssim 0.2$,
\item progressive transition for $h \sim 0.3$,
\item loss of structure for $h \gtrsim 0.5$.
\end{itemize}

Scan over $J_{zz}/J_{xy}$:

\begin{itemize}
\item SO(3)-like regime for $J_{zz}/J_{xy} < 1$,
\item transition near $J_{zz}/J_{xy} \approx 1$,

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation}

\section{Modular Hamiltonians as Geometric Probes}

\subsection{Motivation and derivation chain}

In the BuP framework, an effective Hamiltonian for subsystem dynamics
cannot be postulated --- it must be derived from the global quantum
state. The modular Hamiltonian $K_A = -\log\rho_A$ provides exactly
this derivation, via the chain:

\[
|\Psi\rangle
\;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Tr}_{\bar{A}}}\;
\rho_A
\;\xrightarrow{-\log}\;
K_A
\;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{fit}}\;
H_{\mathrm{eff}}^{1+2}.
\]

This section presents a systematic study of $K_A$ over 180 numerical
configurations ($N \in \{8,10,12\}$, $|A| \in \{3,4,5,6\}$,
$\eta_{\mathrm{micro}} \in \{1.0, 1.1, 1.25, 1.5, 2.0\}$,
three state classes).

\subsection{Model and fit}

We consider spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ chains with XXZ anisotropy:

\[
H = \sum_{\langle i,j\rangle}
\left( J_{xy}(\sigma_i^x\sigma_j^x + \sigma_i^y\sigma_j^y)
+ J_z\,\sigma_i^z\sigma_j^z \right),
\]

with $J_{xy}=1$ fixed and $J_z \in \{1.0, 1.1, 1.25, 1.5, 2.0\}$.

We project $K_A$ onto the 1+2-body Pauli basis:

\[
K_A^{\mathrm{fit}} =
\sum_i \sum_{\alpha} h_i^\alpha\,\sigma_i^\alpha
+ \sum_{i<j}\sum_{\alpha} J_{ij}^{\alpha\alpha}\,\sigma_i^\alpha\sigma_j^\alpha.
\]

Fit quality is measured by $R^2$.

\subsection{Result 1: Universal quasi-locality}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
State class & $\langle R^2\rangle$ & $\sigma_{R^2}$ & $n$ \\
\midrule
Ground  & 0.9948 & 0.0099 & 60 \\
Thermal & 0.9994 & 0.0011 & 60 \\
Random  & 0.9205 & 0.0741 & 60 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Structured states (ground, thermal) are near-perfectly captured
by the 1+2-body expansion. Random states show lower and more variable
fit quality, indicating non-negligible three-body content.

\subsection{Result 2: State-dependent anisotropy amplification}

We define the effective anisotropy and amplification factor:

\[
\eta_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{\langle |J^{zz}|\rangle}
{\tfrac{1}{2}(\langle |J^{xx}|\rangle + \langle |J^{yy}|\rangle)},
\qquad
\mathcal{A} = \frac{\eta_{\mathrm{eff}}}{\eta_{\mathrm{micro}}}.
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
State class & $\langle\mathcal{A}\rangle$ & $\sigma_{\mathcal{A}}$ & $n$ \\
\midrule
Ground  & 1.048 & 0.114 & 60 \\
Thermal & 1.107 & 0.125 & 60 \\
Random  & 0.805 & 0.392 & 60 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The hierarchy

\[
\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{thermal}} \gtrsim \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{ground}} > 1 > \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{random}}
\]

is state-dependent: random states constructed from the same Hamiltonian
suppress anisotropy. The modular Hamiltonian thus acts as a
coherence-sensitive filter. The slightly larger amplification observed
in thermal states compared to ground states should be interpreted
with caution, as it may arise from statistical mixing effects rather
than increased structural coherence.

\subsection{Result 3: Monotonic amplification}

For ground states, $\mathcal{A}$ grows monotonically with
$\eta_{\mathrm{micro}}$:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
$\eta_{\mathrm{micro}}$ & $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{ground}}$ & $\sigma$ \\
\midrule
1.00 & 1.000 & 0.000 \\
1.10 & 1.009 & 0.024 \\
1.25 & 1.026 & 0.057 \\
1.50 & 1.062 & 0.106 \\
2.00 & 1.144 & 0.190 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Random states show no reproducible trend (variance exceeds signal at
all values of $\eta_{\mathrm{micro}}$).

\subsection{Link to modular chaos}

The spectral properties of $K_A$ are consistent with chaotic behavior,
with $\langle r \rangle \in [0.53, 0.59]$, matching GOE/GUE predictions.
This establishes that modular Hamiltonians are not only quasi-local
operators, but also belong to a universal chaotic class, reinforcing
their interpretation as effective dynamical generators.

\subsection{Locality of the modular response}

We perturb the system by $\delta H = \varepsilon\,\sigma_2^z$
($\varepsilon = 0.2$) and measure the response of fitted couplings
as a function of distance from the perturbation:

\[
\mathrm{Loc} = \left\|K_A^{(r=0)}\right\|_F
- \left\langle \left\|K_A^{(r>0)}\right\|_F \right\rangle.
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
State class & $\langle\mathrm{Loc}\rangle$ & $\sigma_{\mathrm{Loc}}$ \\
\midrule
Ground  & $+3.5 \times 10^{-3}$ & $5.8 \times 10^{-3}$ \\
Thermal & $+4.0 \times 10^{-3}$ & $3.8 \times 10^{-3}$ \\
Random  & $-2.8 \times 10^{-3}$ & $2.1 \times 10^{-2}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Structured states show a stable positive locality score. Random states
show no systematic localization pattern (variance exceeds signal).

\subsection{Geometric interpretation}

The couplings $J_{ij}$ extracted from the fit define an interaction
graph that can be interpreted as an emergent geometry. Locality of
$K_A$ corresponds to short-range structure, while anisotropy encodes
directional deformation.

\medskip
The modular Hamiltonian $K_A$ thus provides a concrete operational
bridge between entanglement structure and effective interaction
geometry. Rather than being postulated, the effective couplings
$J_{ij}$ emerge from $K_A$ itself --- they are a derived consequence
of $|\Psi\rangle$, not an input. This is the operational realization
of the BuP principle:

\[
|\Psi\rangle \;\rightarrow\; \rho_A \;\rightarrow\; K_A
\;\rightarrow\; \{J_{ij}\} \;\rightarrow\; \text{effective geometry.}
\]

\subsection{Connection to galactic scales}

In the BuP framework, the scale-dependent dimension $d(r)$ extracted
from galactic rotation curves can be interpreted as a coarse-grained
manifestation of the same entanglement structure probed
microscopically by $K_A$. The state-dependent anisotropy
amplification observed in modular Hamiltonians --- stronger in
structured (low-entropy) states, suppressed in random states ---
is the microscopic counterpart of the regime-dependent effective
dimension observed in SPARC galaxies.

This suggests a continuous derivation chain from quantum to
astrophysical scales:

\[
\text{quantum entanglement}
\;\rightarrow\; K_A \;\rightarrow\; \{J_{ij}\}
\;\rightarrow\; \text{interaction graph}
\;\rightarrow\; d(r)
\;\rightarrow\; \text{rotation curves.}
\]

Agreement with SPARC data therefore provides indirect support for
the microscopic mechanisms identified in this section, and establishes
$K_A$ as the missing link between the quantum physics of subsystems
and the galactic phenomenology of the BuP framework.

\section{Transition Scale Scaling and Lensing Prediction}

\subsection{Motivation}

We seek to identify a scaling structure for the transition parameter
$r_{\mathrm{trans}}$ and test whether this structure propagates to
independent observables such as lensing.

\subsection{Scaling structure of $r_{\mathrm{trans}}$}

A first univariate law gives

\[
r_{\mathrm{trans}} \simeq -5.41 + 1.31\,r_{\max},
\]

with $R^2 \simeq 0.63$.

A more complete multivariate model gives

\[
r_{\mathrm{trans}}
=
-14.06 + 5.39\,r_{\max}
- 4.21\,r_{\mathrm{span}}
+ 0.80\,\frac{v_{b,\max}}{r_{\max}},
\]

with $R^2 \simeq 0.687$ and $\mathrm{RMSE} \simeq 16.50$.

The transition scale thus depends on both the overall system size
and its baryonic compactness.

\subsection{Robustness}

Bootstrap and train/test analysis shows that coefficient signs are
stable, but out-of-sample performance remains scattered
($R^2_{\mathrm{test}} \sim 0.5$). This motivates a regime-based
description.

\subsection{Regime separation}

A split based on baryonic compactness reveals two distinct behaviors:

\begin{itemize}
\item LOW regime: strong dependence on internal structure,
\item HIGH regime: simpler law dominated by $r_{\max}$ and $v_{b,\max}$.
\end{itemize}

In the HIGH regime, the effective law becomes

\[
r_{\mathrm{trans}} \simeq 2.44 + 0.46\,r_{\max} + 0.044\,v_{b,\max},
\]

with $R^2 \simeq 0.50$, suggesting a more rigid emergent geometry
in this regime.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{lensing_regime_profiles.png}
\caption{Mean BuP lensing excess profile by galactic regime.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Lensing prediction}

From the fitted profiles, we construct a deflection proxy

\[
\alpha(R) \propto \frac{v(R)^2}{R},
\]

and define

\[
\Delta \alpha(R) = \alpha_{\mathrm{BuP}}(R) - \alpha_{\mathrm{bary}}(R).
\]

For each galaxy, we extract the peak position

\[
x_{\mathrm{peak}} = \frac{R_{\mathrm{peak}}}{R_{\max}}.
\]

Over 102 galaxies:

\begin{itemize}
\item LOW regime: $x_{\mathrm{peak}} \approx 0.365$
\item HIGH regime: $x_{\mathrm{peak}} \approx 0.498$
\end{itemize}

\[
x_{\mathrm{peak}}^{\mathrm{LOW}} < x_{\mathrm{peak}}^{\mathrm{HIGH}}.
\]

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{lensing_peak_radius_boxplot.png}
\caption{Distribution of $R_{\mathrm{peak}}/R_{\max}$ by galactic regime.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Interpretation}

The BuP framework predicts:

\begin{itemize}
\item LOW regime: more flexible geometry, centralized effect,
\item HIGH regime: more extended geometry, distributed effect.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Physical implication}

This constitutes a falsifiable prediction independent of rotation
curves. At comparable baryonic mass, the radial structure of the
lensing signal depends on the galactic regime.

\subsection{Comparison with dark matter interpretations}

The BuP framework differs conceptually from standard dark halo models.
The gravitational excess is not attributed to an additional invisible
mass component, but to a modification of the emergent geometry itself,
encoded phenomenologically via an effective dimension and a transition
scale $r_{\mathrm{trans}}$.

The lensing proxy results reinforce this distinction. The normalized
radial position of the deflection excess peak differs systematically
between regimes:

\[
\left(\frac{R_{\mathrm{peak}}}{R_{\max}}\right)_{\mathrm{LOW}}
<
\left(\frac{R_{\mathrm{peak}}}{R_{\max}}\right)_{\mathrm{HIGH}}.
\]

The discriminating feature is not only the ability to reproduce
rotation curves, but the prediction of lensing profiles that differ
systematically from dark halo model expectations.

\subsection{Summary}

The regime separation observed in rotation curves propagates to
lensing observables, reinforcing the interpretation of an effective
multi-phase description of emergent geometry.

\section{Conclusion}

Bottom-Up Quantum Gravity proposes that spacetime geometry can emerge
from the entanglement structure of quantum states without any
pre-existing geometric background.

Even small quantum systems ($N=9,16$) exhibit multiple consistent
signatures:

\begin{itemize}
\item emergent dimension tunable by entanglement locality,
\item chaotic modular dynamics with topology-dependent constant $C$,
\item operational ER=EPR shortcuts,
\item algorithmically detectable horizon-like informational bottlenecks,
\item approximate thermodynamic gravity via a Jacobson-type
      entropy-energy relation,
\item local Ollivier--Ricci curvature response to an informational
      perturbation,
\item first phenomenological extension to SPARC galaxies,
\item identification of a scaling structure for $r_{\mathrm{trans}}$
      and separation into two regimes,
\item falsifiable prediction on lensing observables.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip

A complementary important result concerns the internal structure of
the effective BuP vacuum. Our analyses show that this vacuum does not
behave as a trivial isotropic medium, but as a structured medium
whose properties depend on interaction anisotropy and the level of
local disorder. In particular, we observe the robust selection of a
U(1)-like effective symmetry, associated with the selection of a
privileged direction in the space of local generators of the emergent
gauge connection.

This residual symmetry is not the result of spontaneous breaking in
the field-theoretic sense --- local fields and the anisotropy
$J_{zz} \neq J_{xy}$ already break $SO(3)$ explicitly at the
Hamiltonian level. It results from a \textbf{robust effective axis
selection} by the ground state, whose robustness with respect to
graph reconstruction choices (kNN parameter $k \in \{1,\ldots,7\}$)
indicates that it is an intrinsic property of the state, and not a
numerical artifact. The controlled transition toward SO(3) is observed
around $J_{zz}/J_{xy} \approx 1$ and $h \approx 0.3$.

This emergence is controlled by two parameters: the anisotropy ratio
$J_{zz}/J_{xy}$ and the local disorder amplitude $h$. A sharp
transition is observed around $J_{zz}/J_{xy} \approx 1$ and
$h \approx 0.3$, beyond which the effective symmetry reverts to SO(3).

The systematic study of modular Hamiltonians further establishes that
$K_A$ is quasi-local ($R^2 \geq 0.99$ for structured states) and
amplifies microscopic anisotropy in a state-dependent manner
($\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{ground}} = 1.048 \pm 0.114$), confirming
their role as geometric probes of entanglement structure. The
modular Hamiltonian provides a concrete operational bridge: rather
than postulating effective couplings, the BuP framework derives them
directly from $|\Psi\rangle$ through the chain
$|\Psi\rangle \to \rho_A \to K_A \to \{J_{ij}\}$.

Thus, the Bottom-Up program does not only lead to emergent geometry
and gravity, but also to effective symmetry structures and derived
effective Hamiltonians --- opening the way toward a unified
understanding where geometry, dynamics, and symmetry emerge jointly
from the informational organization of quantum states.
